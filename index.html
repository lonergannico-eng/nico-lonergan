About

Researching coastal change from above and below

I am a master’s student in the Department of Oceanography & Coastal Sciences at Louisiana State University. My research sits at the intersection of coastal restoration, wetland biogeochemistry, and climate-driven ecosystem stress. I study how black mangrove expansion, thin-layer placement of dredged sediment, drought, and hurricane-related storm surge influence nutrient cycling, salinity exposure, and vegetation condition across Louisiana wetlands.

My work combines field sampling, laboratory nutrient analyses, geospatial methods, and satellite-based environmental monitoring. I am especially interested in producing science that supports restoration planning and helps explain how environmental change affects both wetland function and long-term coastal resilience.

Research

Current projects

Thin-layer placement and emerging mangrove ecosystems

This project examines how thin-layer placement of dredged sediment affects short-term soil biogeochemistry in Louisiana’s expanding black mangrove wetlands. The work evaluates responses such as denitrification, nutrient fluxes, redox conditions, and pH across sediment treatments, with the goal of informing wetland restoration design under rapid coastal change.

Drought, storm surge, and coastal wetland health

This project combines remote sensing products with long-term salinity records to compare how drought-driven hypersalinity and hurricane storm surge affect vegetation stress in coastal wetlands. The work helps clarify whether prolonged drought may drive more persistent ecological damage than short-lived storm events.
